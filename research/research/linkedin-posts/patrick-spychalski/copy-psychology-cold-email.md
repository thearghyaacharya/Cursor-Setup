## Post Content

Cold email is a direct response copywriting problem.
Most people treat it like a formal business communication problem.

The difference:

Direct response copy is engineered to trigger a specific action.
Every word either earns its place or gets cut.

The psychological principles that drive cold email replies:

1. Pattern Interrupt
   The inbox is noise. Your subject line must break the pattern.
   Not with gimmicks — with specificity.
   "[Company] + [Competitor] + [Result]" outperforms 
   "Quick question" every time.

2. Loss Aversion Over Gain Framing
   "Most [ICP] are losing X because of Y" 
   outperforms "We can help you gain X."
   People act to avoid loss before they act to acquire gain.

3. Social Proof as Risk Reducer
   Name a recognizable company they'd respect.
   "[Competitor] reduced their CAC by 32% using this."
   Social proof isn't bragging — it's risk reduction for the buyer.

4. Micro-Commitment CTA
   "Does this resonate?" gets more replies than "Book a 30-minute call."
   Small ask = low resistance = high conversion.

5. Specificity Over Vagueness
   "We help SaaS companies grow" means nothing.
   "We help Series A SaaS companies reduce churn in the first 90 days" 
   speaks to someone exactly.

## Why This Was Selected

Applies direct response copywriting principles to cold email — 
a framework that elevates the craft from template-filling to 
psychological precision. Each principle is immediately testable.

## Key Insight

Cold email copy fails when it's written as business communication. 
It works when it's written as direct response — engineered 
specifically to reduce resistance and trigger one micro-action: a reply.
